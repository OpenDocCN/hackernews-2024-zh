**Francisco Javier Varela García** (September 7, 1946 – May 28, 2001) was a [Chilean](/wiki/Chile "Chile") [biologist](/wiki/Biologist "Biologist"), [philosopher](/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy"), [cybernetician](/wiki/Cybernetician "Cybernetician"), and [neuroscientist](/wiki/Neuroscientist "Neuroscientist") who, together with his mentor [Humberto Maturana](/wiki/Humberto_Maturana "Humberto Maturana"), is best known for introducing the concept of [autopoiesis](/wiki/Autopoiesis "Autopoiesis") to biology, and for co-founding the [Mind and Life Institute](/wiki/Mind_and_Life_Institute "Mind and Life Institute") to promote dialog between science and [Buddhism](/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism").

## Life and career[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Varela&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Life and career")]

Varela was born in 1946 in [Talcahuano](/wiki/Talcahuano "Talcahuano") in Chile, the son of Corina María Elena García Tapia and Raúl Andrés Varela Rodríguez.^([[1]](#cite_note-1))^([[2]](#cite_note-2)) After completing secondary school at the Liceo Alemán del Verbo Divino in [Santiago](/wiki/Santiago "Santiago") (1951–1963), like his mentor [Humberto Maturana](/wiki/Humberto_Maturana "Humberto Maturana"), Varela temporarily studied medicine at the [Pontifical Catholic University of Chile](/wiki/Pontifical_Catholic_University_of_Chile "Pontifical Catholic University of Chile") and graduated with a degree in biology from the [University of Chile](/wiki/University_of_Chile "University of Chile"). He later obtained a Ph.D. in biology at [Harvard University](/wiki/Harvard_University "Harvard University"). His thesis, defended in 1970 and supervised by [Torsten Wiesel](/wiki/Torsten_Wiesel "Torsten Wiesel"), was titled *Insect Retinas: Information processing in the compound eye*.

After the [1973 military coup](/wiki/1973_Chilean_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1973 Chilean coup d'état") led by [Augusto Pinochet](/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet "Augusto Pinochet"), Varela and his family spent 7 years in [exile](/wiki/Exile "Exile") in the United States before he returned to Chile to become a professor of biology at the Universidad de Chile.

Varela became familiar, by practice, with [Tibetan Buddhism](/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism "Tibetan Buddhism") in the 1970s, initially studying, together with [Keun-Tshen Goba](/w/index.php?title=Keun-Tshen_Goba&action=edit&redlink=1 "Keun-Tshen Goba (page does not exist)") (*né* Ezequiel Hernandez Urdaneta), with the meditation master [Chögyam Trungpa Rinpoche](/wiki/Ch%C3%B6gyam_Trungpa_Rinpoche "Chögyam Trungpa Rinpoche"), founder of [Vajradhatu](/wiki/Vajradhatu "Vajradhatu") and [Shambhala Training](/wiki/Shambhala_Training "Shambhala Training"), and later with [Tulku Urgyen Rinpoche](/wiki/Tulku_Urgyen_Rinpoche "Tulku Urgyen Rinpoche").

In 1986, he settled in [France](/wiki/France "France"), where he first taught cognitive science and epistemology at the [École Polytechnique](/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique "École Polytechnique"), and later neuroscience at the [University of Paris](/wiki/University_of_Paris "University of Paris"). From 1988 until his death, he led a research group, as Director of Research at the [CNRS](/wiki/CNRS "CNRS") (Centre National de Recherche Scientifique).

In 1987, Varela, along with [R. Adam Engle](/wiki/R._Adam_Engle "R. Adam Engle"), founded the [Mind and Life Institute](/wiki/Mind_and_Life_Institute "Mind and Life Institute"), initially to sponsor a series of dialogues between scientists and [the Dalai Lama](/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama "14th Dalai Lama") about the relationship between modern science and [Buddhism](/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism").^([[3]](#cite_note-3)) The Institute continues today as a major nexus for such dialog as well as promoting and supporting multidisciplinary scientific investigation in mind sciences, contemplative scholarship and practice and related areas in the interface of science with [meditation](/wiki/Meditation "Meditation") and other [contemplative practices](/wiki/Contemplative "Contemplative"), especially [Buddhist practices](/wiki/Buddhist_meditation "Buddhist meditation").^([[4]](#cite_note-4))

Varela died in 2001 in [Paris](/wiki/Paris "Paris") of [Hepatitis C](/wiki/Hepatitis_C "Hepatitis C") after having written an account of his 1998 liver transplant.^([[5]](#cite_note-5)) Varela had four children, including the actress, environmental spokesperson, and model [Leonor Varela](/wiki/Leonor_Varela "Leonor Varela").

## Work and legacy[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Varela&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Work and legacy")]

Varela was trained as a biologist, mathematician and philosopher through the influence of different teachers, [Humberto Maturana](/wiki/Humberto_Maturana "Humberto Maturana") and [Torsten Wiesel](/wiki/Torsten_Wiesel "Torsten Wiesel").

He wrote and edited a number of books and numerous journal articles in [biology](/wiki/Biology "Biology"), [neurology](/wiki/Neurology "Neurology"), [cognitive science](/wiki/Cognitive_science "Cognitive science"), [mathematics](/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), and [philosophy](/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy"). He founded, with others, the [Integral Institute](/wiki/Integral_Institute "Integral Institute"), a [thinktank](/wiki/Thinktank "Thinktank") dedicated to the cross-fertilization of ideas and disciplines.

Varela supported [embodied philosophy](/wiki/Embodied_philosophy "Embodied philosophy"), viewing human [cognition](/wiki/Cognition "Cognition") and [consciousness](/wiki/Consciousness "Consciousness") in terms of the [enactive structures](/wiki/Enaction "Enaction") in which they arise. These comprise the body (as a biological system and as personally experienced) and the physical world which it enacts.^([[6]](#cite_note-6))

Varela's work popularized within the field of neuroscience the concept of [neurophenomenology](/wiki/Neurophenomenology "Neurophenomenology"). This concept combined the phenomenology of [Edmund Husserl](/wiki/Edmund_Husserl "Edmund Husserl") and of [Maurice Merleau-Ponty](/wiki/Maurice_Merleau-Ponty "Maurice Merleau-Ponty"), with "first-person science." Neurophenomenology requires observers to examine their own conscious experience using scientifically verifiable methods.

In the 1996 popular book *The Web of Life: A New Scientific Understanding of Living Systems*, physicist [Fritjof Capra](/wiki/Fritjof_Capra "Fritjof Capra") makes extensive reference to Varela and Maturana's theory of [autopoiesis](/wiki/Autopoiesis "Autopoiesis") as part of a new, [systems-based](/wiki/Systems_thinking "Systems thinking") scientific approach for describing the interrelationships and interdependence of psychological, biological, physical, social, and cultural phenomena.^([[7]](#cite_note-7)) Written for a general audience, *The Web of Life* helped popularize the work of Varela and Maturana, as well as that of [Ilya Prigogine](/wiki/Ilya_Prigogine "Ilya Prigogine") and [Gregory Bateson](/wiki/Gregory_Bateson "Gregory Bateson").^([[8]](#cite_note-8))

Varela's 1991 book *The Embodied Mind: Cognitive Science and Human Experience*, co-authored with [Evan Thompson](/wiki/Evan_Thompson "Evan Thompson") and [Eleanor Rosch](/wiki/Eleanor_Rosch "Eleanor Rosch"), is considered a classic in the field of cognitive science, offering pioneering phenomenological connections and introducing the Buddhism-informed [enactivist](/wiki/Enactivist "Enactivist") and [embodied cognition](/wiki/Embodied_cognition "Embodied cognition") approach.^([[9]](#cite_note-9)) A revised edition of *The Embodied Mind* was published in 2017, featuring substantive introductions by the surviving authors, as well as a preface by [Jon Kabat-Zinn](/wiki/Jon_Kabat-Zinn "Jon Kabat-Zinn").^([[10]](#cite_note-10))
